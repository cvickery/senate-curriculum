February 17, 2012

 

Dear Professor Alfar,

Thank you for your message concerning the 3-credit/3-hour courses for the Common Core and, in particular, the part of the Common Core concerning English Composition.

As you undoubtedly know, the Common Core was designed by a Task Force of 47 faculty, 4 campus-based administrators, and 4 students.  The Task Force necessarily worked within the parameters specified by the June 2011 Board of Trustees resolution:  30 credits for the Common Core for all colleges, and 6-12 additional general education credits for the senior colleges (the College Option).

In the draft of the Common Core that the Task Force released for comment, the courses were a mix of 3 and 4 credits, including a total of 7 credits for English Composition.  However, in their final recommendations the Task Force changed to all 3-credit courses, with English Composition totaling 6 credits.  In this way the committee changed from recommending 9 courses of unequal weights to 10 courses of equal weight.  The Task Force's final recommendations are located at:

http://www1.cuny.edu/mu/ academic-news/files/2011/12/ CommonCoreStructureFinalRec. pdf

Also, it was clear in the final recommendations of the Task Force that they considered enhancement of writing skills to be extremely important for students.  For this reason, in addition to designating 6 of 30 Common Core credits for English Composition, they also made it apparent that they considered the further development of writing skills to be extremely important for undergraduates, beyond the basic courses in English Composition.  This is shown by their specification of the following learning outcome for the entire flexible portion of the Common Core (which consists of 6 courses of 3 credits each, for a total of 18 credits):

"Produce well-reasoned written or oral arguments using evidence to support conclusions."

The Task Force also designed the Common Core so as to further develop students' research skills beyond those learned in English Composition.  This is shown by their specification of the following learning outcomes for the entire flexible portion of the Common Core (6 courses of 3 credits each, for a total of 18 credits):

"Gather, interpret, and assess information from a variety of sources and points of view."
"Evaluate evidence and arguments critically or analytically."

In addition to the skills to be gained in our students by the 6 credits of English Composition, and the additional work on these skills within the 18 credits of the Flexible Core, CUNY's new General Education Framework offers still more opportunities for students to be taught writing skills, should the colleges wish to do so.

First, certain courses within the Common Core and/or the College Option, may be designated as writing intensive courses, and students may be required to take a certain number of these courses.

Second, senior colleges may wish to use their College Option credits for explicit additional writing instruction for students--either as a full three- or four-credit course for students who have completed freshman composition, or as a one-credit add-on to one or both of the freshman composition courses so that these courses become 4-credit, 4-hour courses.

There is variability both within and outside of CUNY as to how English Composition is taught in terms of credits and hours of course meetings.  I hope that this message has provided you with some information concerning the ways that English Composition, and other forms of writing instruction, can be offered within CUNY's new General Education Framework. Instruction in how to write well is extremely important to our students and I greatly appreciate your expressing your concerns and those of the English Discipline Council.

Sincerely,
Lexa Logue

Alexandra W. Logue
Executive Vice Chancellor and University Provost
The City University of New York
